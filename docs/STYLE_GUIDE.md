# Content Style Guide

## Voice

- Plain English first. Define legal terms when they appear.
- Active voice. Short sentences at L1; longer at L3.
- No partisan cheerleading. Present evidence; let readers think.

## Depth Levels

| Level | Length | Tone | Format |
|-------|--------|------|--------|
| L1 | ~150 words | Conversational | One idea, no jargon |
| L2 | ~800 words | Article | Subheads, one visual optional |
| L3 | ~3000 words | Explainer | Charts, timeline links, cross-refs |
| L4 | Unlimited | Research | Sources, cases, data tables, PDFs |

## Structure Rules

1. Every hall page opens with **purpose** (one sentence).
2. Every factual claim in L2+ links to L4 or claims ledger.
3. Debate content always presents **both sides** before synthesis.
4. Reform content explains what each proposal **would and would not do**.
5. Live state content (Montana, Hawaii) dated and sourced.

## Words to Avoid at L1

- Strict scrutiny, compelling interest (use at L2+ with explanation)
- "Destroy democracy" / "buy elections" (too loaded for L1)
- Unsourced superlatives ("unprecedented," "massive" without data)

## Words to Use

- Independent expenditure, outside spending, disclosure
- Artificial person / corporate charter (when discussing state reform)
- Majority opinion, dissent (when discussing the case)

## Headlines

- Questions work at Level 0: "What happened?" "Why does it matter?"
- Declarative at halls: "Hall 3: What Changed After 2010"
