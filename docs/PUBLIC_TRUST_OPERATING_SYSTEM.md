**Public Trust Operating System v1.0**

---

# Purpose

The most valuable asset of the Citizens United Facts platform is not its technology.

It is public trust.

Every design decision, article, chart, graphic, timeline, educational resource, and community presentation should strengthen the reader's confidence that the platform is accurate, transparent, intellectually honest, and open about its sources.

This framework establishes the standards by which trust is earned and maintained.

Mission Control should treat trust as a measurable project objective.

---

# Trust Philosophy

The platform follows one central principle:

> **"Don't ask readers to trust us. Show them why they can verify what we present."**

The platform should continually invite readers to examine the evidence for themselves.

---

# The Trust Pyramid

## Level One — Clear Language

Readers should understand what is being explained.

Avoid unnecessary jargon.

Legal terminology should always be accompanied by plain-language explanations.

---

## Level Two — Transparent Sources

Readers should always know:

* Where information came from.
* When it was published.
* Who produced it.
* Whether it is a primary or secondary source.

---

## Level Three — Evidence Before Conclusions

Whenever practical, present:

* The historical record.
* The court opinion.
* The dataset.
* The statute.

Then explain what it means.

Readers should be able to distinguish source material from explanatory text.

---

## Level Four — Honest Context

Many issues surrounding *Citizens United* are debated.

The platform should clearly identify:

* Established facts.
* Legal holdings.
* Historical interpretations.
* Empirical findings.
* Areas where scholars or policymakers disagree.

Presenting disagreement openly strengthens credibility.

---

## Level Five — Continuous Review

Trust requires maintenance.

Every major educational resource should have a scheduled review cycle.

Mission Control should identify materials due for review.

---

# Source Transparency Standards

Every educational page should display:

* Last reviewed date.
* Primary sources used.
* Supporting references.
* Citation count.
* Related documents.
* Version history.

Readers should never wonder where information originated.

---

# Evidence Levels

The platform should classify supporting material.

## Level A

Primary legal materials.

Examples:

* Supreme Court opinions.
* Federal statutes.
* Arkansas statutes.
* Official government publications.

---

## Level B

Official public records and datasets.

Examples:

* Election spending reports.
* Government statistics.
* Court filings.

---

## Level C

Peer-reviewed academic scholarship.

Examples:

* Law review articles.
* Academic journals.
* University publications.

---

## Level D

Reputable explanatory sources.

Examples:

* Educational organizations.
* Major news organizations.
* Think tank reports.

These should supplement, not replace, primary sources.

---

# Fact Review Process

Every factual claim should move through a structured process.

1. Drafted.
2. Supported by evidence.
3. Source reviewed.
4. Editorial reviewed.
5. Published.
6. Scheduled for future review.

Mission Control should display review status.

---

# Transparency Panels

Major pages should include expandable panels such as:

## How We Know This

Explains the evidence supporting the page.

---

## What This Page Does Not Claim

Clarifies common misunderstandings and limits of the discussion.

---

## Questions Still Being Studied

Highlights areas where research or public debate continues.

These panels reinforce intellectual honesty.

---

# Version History

Important pages should maintain a visible revision history.

Readers should be able to see:

* Publication date.
* Major updates.
* Reason for updates.
* Research revisions.

Transparency over time builds confidence.

---

# Editorial Independence

Educational content should be reviewed according to the platform's published editorial standards.

Changes should be documented, and significant revisions should note why they were made.

The platform's credibility depends on consistent editorial discipline.

---

# Reader Feedback

Visitors should be able to:

* Report possible factual errors.
* Suggest additional sources.
* Recommend clarifications.
* Ask educational questions.

Submissions should enter an editorial review process before changes are made.

---

# Mission Control Trust Dashboard

Mission Control should report:

* Pages fully sourced.
* Pages under review.
* Facts verified.
* Sources added.
* Broken citations.
* Scheduled reviews completed.
* Reader feedback received.
* Corrections published.

Trust becomes measurable rather than assumed.

---

# Public Accountability

The platform should maintain public pages describing:

* Editorial standards.
* Research methodology.
* Citation policy.
* Review process.
* Correction policy.
* Privacy policy.
* Accessibility commitment.

Readers should understand how the platform operates.

---

# Long-Term Goal

Citizens United Facts should become known for one defining characteristic:

**If a claim appears on the platform, readers know they can examine the supporting evidence, understand the context, and see how the conclusion was reached.**

That reputation should become the institution's greatest strength.

---

# Governing Principle

The platform earns trust through transparency.

Every article, chart, timeline, lesson, and educational resource should help readers verify information rather than simply accept it.

The highest standard of civic education is not asking people to believe—it is giving them the tools to investigate, understand, and reach informed conclusions based on reliable evidence.
