Hall 7: Montana and Hawaii

The live battlefield — one ballot initiative path, one legislative test-case path. Montana = people-powered democracy. Hawaii = legislature-enacted corporate law.

Montana is pursuing a 2026 ballot initiative (Initiative 194) to prohibit "artificial persons" from political spending. Hawaii enacted Act 11 (signed May 2026, effective July 2027) using corporate-charter theory to restrict corporate election spending. Both test what states can do after Citizens United — one through voters, one through the legislature.

Montana — Ballot Path

Initiative 194

"Exclude Political Spending from Artificial Persons' Charter Powers Initiative" would prohibit artificial persons from contributing to campaigns, ballot measures, or political parties.

Signatures submitted June 2026. County verification due to the state by July 17, 2026.

Theory: Artificial person / corporate charter powers.

Risks: First Amendment challenge likely if qualified.

Hawaii — Legislative Path

Act 11

Hawaii is not a citizen initiative state like Montana. Act 11 (signed May 2026) restricts corporations and artificial entities from election spending via state corporate law.

Effective July 1, 2027. Expected First Amendment litigation.

Supporters: Test state for undoing Citizens United through corporate law.

Critics: Likely conflicts with Supreme Court precedent.

Bottom line: Montana = ballot initiative path. Hawaii = legislative test-case path. The right comparison for understanding reform after Citizens United.

Montana Drill-downs

  • Ballot initiative path — Voters can qualify statewide measures; signatures verified by counties.
  • Artificial person theory — Restrict spending by treating it as outside corporate charter authority.
  • Signature process — Submitted June 2026; county certification deadline July 17, 2026.
  • Legal challenge risks — Preemption and First Amendment challenges expected if passed.
  • Messaging lessons — Frame as anti-corporate influence, not anti-speech.

Hawaii Drill-downs

  • Legislative path — No ballot initiative; lawmakers enacted reform directly.
  • Act 11 — Signed May 2026; effective July 1, 2027.
  • Corporate-charter theory — State regulates corporations through charter/power law.
  • Expected litigation — Constitutional challenge anticipated.
  • Lessons for other states — Legislative route available even without initiative process.
